Hearing Aids and Over-the-Counter Options: Clinical Practices and Technology Integration

Date: March 17, 2025
Presenter: Elizabeth Rivera Rosario, AuD
Affiliation: Doctoral Student, Department of Communicative Disorders and Sciences, University at Buffalo

Session Overview

Our fourth Audiology Workshop featured Elizabeth Rivera Rosario, AuD, a doctoral student in the Department of Communicative Disorders and Sciences at the University at Buffalo. This session explored the clinical aspects of hearing aids, the emergence of over-the-counter (OTC) options, and the technological integration opportunities at this intersection.

Hearing Aids: Clinical Impact and Benefits

Elizabeth began with a comprehensive overview of hearing aid technology and its critical role in managing hearing loss. She emphasized three key benefits of properly fitted hearing aids:

  • Quality of Life Improvements: Addressing social isolation and communication difficulties
  • Cognitive Benefits: Reducing cognitive load associated with hearing impairment
  • Speech Recognition: Enhancing the ability to understand speech in various environments

The presentation highlighted the importance of proper fitting and verification in achieving these benefits, particularly through real ear measurements that ensure appropriate amplification is delivered.

Traditional vs. Over-the-Counter Hearing Aids

Elizabeth outlined the key differences between traditional audiologist-fitted hearing aids and the emerging over-the-counter options:

Traditional Hearing Aids Over-the-Counter (OTC) Hearing Aids
Professional assessment and fitting Self-assessment and self-fitting
Real ear measurement verification No verification or limited self-verification
Customized programming Limited pre-set programming options
Higher cost ($1,000-$6,000 per pair) Lower cost ($200-$1,000 per pair)
Bundled follow-up services Limited or no professional support

Elizabeth noted that while OTC options have made hearing assistance more accessible, research suggests traditionally fitted devices may provide superior outcomes for many users, particularly those with complex hearing needs.

Real Ear Measurement Process

A significant portion of the session focused on real ear measurement (REM), a critical verification step in the hearing aid fitting process:

Elizabeth explained the process in detail:

  1. A tiny microphone is placed in the ear canal
  2. Sound is played at different frequencies and intensities
  3. Measurements are taken with and without the hearing aid
  4. The difference represents the actual gain provided by the device
  5. Adjustments are made to match prescribed targets based on the individual's audiogram

Wei Sun clarified that this process measures the true output of the hearing aid when worn by the specific individual, accounting for the unique acoustics of each person's ear canal. The team discussed that this process typically takes 40-60 minutes with a human subject, while coupler measurements (without a human) might take 15-20 minutes but lack personalization.

Anatomical Considerations in Hearing Aid Fitting

The discussion addressed how individual ear canal anatomy significantly impacts hearing aid performance:

  • Each person's ear canal creates unique resonance patterns
  • These resonances can amplify certain frequencies while diminishing others
  • The shape of the ear canal changes throughout life and even during the day
  • These variations significantly impact the effective gain of hearing aids

Varun and Wei Sun highlighted that these anatomical differences explain why generic amplification (as often provided by OTC devices) may not deliver optimal results for many individuals with hearing loss.

Discussion: Technology Integration Opportunities

The session sparked several discussions about potential technological innovations at the intersection of AI and hearing healthcare:

3D Printing Applications

The team explored using 3D printing technology for hearing aid calibration and verification. Possibilities included:

  • Creating standardized ear canal models for testing
  • Using 3D scanning to produce custom ear molds
  • Developing personalized coupler systems that replicate individual ear acoustics

AI-Enhanced Verification

Discussion centered on how AI might streamline the verification process:

  • Automated gain adjustment based on real-time measurements
  • Pattern recognition to identify problematic frequency responses
  • Predictive modeling of individual-specific acoustic properties

Improving OTC Experience

The team considered ways that technology could improve the OTC hearing aid experience:

  • Smartphone-based verification tools
  • Self-administered hearing tests with AI validation
  • Remote assistance platforms connecting consumers with professionals

Professional Impact of OTC Hearing Aids

Elizabeth discussed how the audiology profession is adapting to the OTC hearing aid market:

  • Initial resistance giving way to recognition of inevitability due to FDA approval
  • Clinics developing new service models focused on OTC support and verification
  • Concerns about revenue impacts, as traditional bundled hearing aid sales represent a significant income source for practices
  • Emerging opportunities for audiologists to serve as consultants rather than device providers

The group discussed conducting research on audiologists' current perspectives on OTC devices and their impact on clinical practice, with plans to design a survey for local Buffalo audiology clinics.

Future Directions

The workshop identified several promising research directions at the intersection of hearing aid technology, clinical practice, and AI:

  1. Verification Tools: Developing accessible verification methods for OTC devices
  2. Economic Models: Exploring new business models for audiology practices in the OTC era
  3. Hybrid Approaches: Creating systems that combine the accessibility of OTC with the precision of professional fitting
  4. Custom Manufacturing: Investigating cost-effective personalization through advanced manufacturing techniques
